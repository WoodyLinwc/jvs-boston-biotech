# Facility Design

Biomanufacturing facilities are purpose-built environments designed to support the safe, consistent, and compliant production of biological products. Unlike general laboratory or industrial spaces, biotech manufacturing facilities must meet strict regulatory requirements for contamination control, environmental monitoring, and personnel flow.

## Why Facility Design Matters

In biomanufacturing, the facility itself is part of the process control system. A poorly designed facility creates contamination risks that cannot be fixed downstream. Regulators (FDA, EMA) inspect facility design during manufacturing license applications and routine audits — design flaws can result in warning letters, shutdowns, or batch failures.

## Cleanroom Classifications

Cleanrooms are rated by the number of airborne particles allowed per cubic meter of air. Different manufacturing activities require different cleanliness levels:

- **ISO 5 / EU Grade A** — the highest cleanliness level, used for aseptic filling, open product containers, and any critical zone where the product is directly exposed
- **ISO 7 / EU Grade B** — the background environment immediately surrounding a Grade A zone during aseptic operations
- **ISO 7 / EU Grade C** — used for preparation of solutions intended for aseptic filling and less critical sterile processing steps
- **ISO 8 / EU Grade D** — less critical support areas such as gowning rooms and buffer preparation

Cell culture and chromatography are typically performed in Grade C or D areas. Final fill-finish — filling drug product into vials or syringes — requires Grade A within a Grade B background.

## HVAC and Air Handling

The HVAC (Heating, Ventilation, and Air Conditioning) system is one of the most critical utility systems in a biomanufacturing facility:

- **HEPA Filtration** — High-Efficiency Particulate Air filters remove ≥99.97% of particles ≥0.3 µm from supply air
- **Air Changes Per Hour (ACPH)** — higher-grade rooms require more frequent air changes; Grade A zones use unidirectional (laminar) airflow, while Grade B areas typically require 40–60 ACPH
- **Pressure Cascades** — rooms are maintained at differential pressures relative to each other; product rooms are typically positive pressure to push air outward and keep contamination out, while containment areas may be negative pressure to keep biological material in
- **Temperature and Humidity Control** — critical for personnel comfort in gowning and for product stability in processing areas

## Personnel and Material Flow

One of the most important design principles is **unidirectional flow** — people, materials, and waste should move in one direction to prevent cross-contamination:

- **Personnel airlocks** — gowning rooms where staff progressively don cleanroom garments before entering manufacturing areas
- **Material airlocks (pass-throughs)** — interlocked chambers that prevent both doors from opening simultaneously, used to transfer materials into cleanrooms without breaking the room's environmental integrity
- **Waste corridors** — separate routes for removing waste and used materials so they never cross paths with incoming clean materials

## Key Construction and Surface Requirements

- **Smooth, coved surfaces** — walls, floors, and ceilings must have no cracks, joints, or ledges where particles or microorganisms can accumulate; coved (rounded) floor-to-wall junctions are standard
- **Cleanable materials** — stainless steel, epoxy flooring, and coated panels that can withstand repeated cleaning and disinfection with harsh chemical agents
- **Minimized penetrations** — pipes, conduits, and utilities that pass through walls must be sealed to maintain room integrity and pressure differentials

## Critical Utilities

Biomanufacturing facilities rely on several specialized utility systems:

- **WFI (Water for Injection)** — ultrapure water used for preparing buffers, media, and rinsing equipment; maintained in a hot circulating loop (≥80°C) to prevent biofilm formation
- **Clean Steam** — used for sterilization-in-place (SIP) of equipment and vessels
- **Process Gases** — CO₂ for pH control in bioreactors, O₂ and N₂ for sparging and blanketing, and compressed air, all delivered at controlled purity levels
- **Clean-in-Place (CIP) Systems** — automated systems that clean tanks, bioreactors, and piping in place using caustic and acid cycles, without requiring disassembly

## Containment Considerations

For certain biological agents — such as viral vectors or live attenuated vaccines — facilities must also provide **biological containment** to protect personnel and the environment. This involves negative-pressure rooms, dedicated HVAC exhaust with HEPA filtration, and validated decontamination systems for liquid waste before it leaves the facility.
